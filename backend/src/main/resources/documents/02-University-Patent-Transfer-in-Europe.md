# University Patent Transfer in Europe

## Overview

This article examines how European universities transfer and commercialise their patented research, and the role of technology transfer offices.

---

#### What share of European inventions come from universities?

Universities and public research organisations generate over 10% of all European patent applications filed at the EPO. More than 1,200 European universities have filed patent applications at the EPO between 2000 and 2020, with applications increasing steadily over that period. This reflects a shift towards institutional ownership and proactive commercialisation of academic inventions.

#### How do European universities prefer to commercialise their patents?

Licensing is the main route, accounting for around 70% of commercialised inventions, followed by R&D cooperation (14%) and patent sales (9%). Universities and PROs tend to commercialise primarily in their home markets, though institutions in southern and eastern Europe more often seek partners in other European countries. The Unitary Patent system, in force since 2023, is intended to support cross-border knowledge circulation and commercialisation.

#### What is the role of Technology Transfer Offices (TTOs) in Europe?

TTOs manage IP portfolios, support licensing and spin-off creation, and connect researchers with industry. European TTOs are represented by ASTP (Association of Science and Technology Transfer Professionals), with 800+ members in 41 countries. The European TTO Circle brings together 35 major research and technology organisations, representing some 200,000 researchers, 35,000 patents, and 4,000 startups. TTOs are essential for turning academic innovations into market impact.

#### What challenges do European university patent transfers face?

Despite progress, universities operate mainly within national systems with limited cross-border linkages. This fragmentation hinders a unified European market for research and technology transfer. Institutional differences, insufficient valuation capacity, and cultural gaps between academia and industry also affect commercialisation rates. The Unitary Patent and Unified Patent Court aim to reduce complexity and improve cross-border commercialisation.

#### What best practices support university patent commercialisation in Europe?

Successful institutions combine: clear IP policies, professional TTOs, early market and commercialisation assessment, structured licensing programmes, spin-off support, and integration with national and EU innovation ecosystems. Collaboration between universities, industry, and policy actors, along with tools such as the Horizon Results Platform, helps identify exploitation partners and accelerate commercialisation.
