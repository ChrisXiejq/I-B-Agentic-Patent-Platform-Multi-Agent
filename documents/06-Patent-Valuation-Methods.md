# Patent Valuation Methods

## Overview

This article describes the main approaches and practices for valuing patents in the European context.

---

#### What are the three main patent valuation methods?

Three primary approaches are widely used: (1) **Cost method** – value based on development and patenting costs; (2) **Market method** – value from comparable transactions; (3) **Income method** – present value of expected future cash flows. The cost method is simple but ignores commercial potential; the market method needs comparable data; the income method is often preferred but relies on uncertain projections. Choice depends on purpose (e.g. licensing, M&A, collateral, litigation).

#### How does the income method work for patent valuation?

The income method estimates the present value of future economic benefits (e.g. licence royalties or product profits) attributable to the patent. Cash flows are projected over the patent’s remaining life, adjusted for risk, and discounted to present value. Key inputs include revenue forecasts, royalty rates, profit margins, and discount rates. It is often used for licensing and investment decisions but is sensitive to assumptions.

#### Are there European standards for patent valuation?

Several standards and frameworks apply. Germany’s DIN 77100 provides guidance for monetary patent valuation. European practice often combines technology, legal, and commercial factors, industry benchmark royalty rates, and risk modelling. The European Commission’s IP Helpdesk recognises valuation as important for licensing, assignments, and IP-backed financing. Professional certifications such as CPVA (Certified Patent Valuation Analyst) support consistent methodology.

#### What qualitative factors affect patent value in Europe?

Factors include: **technical strength** – novelty, inventiveness, scope; **legal strength** – validity, enforceability, freedom to operate; **market factors** – demand, competition, regulation; **strategic fit** – alignment with business and portfolio; **remaining term** – time left until expiry; **geographic coverage** – countries where protected. The Unitary Patent can increase value by simplifying European coverage and enforcement.

#### When is patent valuation needed for commercialisation?

Valuation is typically needed for: licensing negotiations, patent sales or acquisitions, equity investments and spin-offs, IP-backed lending and collateral, litigation and damages, transfer pricing, and financial reporting. Horizon Europe beneficiaries may need valuation when transferring or licensing project results. Using a recognised methodology and qualified professionals improves credibility with partners, investors, and lenders.
