# IP-Backed Financing and Patent Collateral

## Overview

This article discusses how patents and other IP can be used as collateral and for financing in Europe.

---

#### What is IP-backed lending and patent collateral?

IP-backed lending uses intellectual property (patents, trademarks, designs) as collateral to secure loans. It allows companies to obtain debt financing without giving up equity and is especially relevant when tangible assets are limited. In Europe, intangibles can represent 70–80% of firm value, yet traditional lending still focuses on physical collateral. Patent pledges enable firms to access additional debt capacity based on their innovation assets.

#### What evidence exists for patent collateral in European countries?

Research shows significant effects: **France** – firms pledge trademarks (72%), patents (26%), and designs (2%); IP pledges improve access to debt, especially for small, constrained firms, and correlate with higher growth. **Sweden and Netherlands** – patent pledging allows firms to borrow roughly 21% (Sweden) and 26% (Netherlands) more; Dutch and Swedish firms could theoretically access over €7–10 billion in additional debt if full patent portfolios were pledged. **UK** – IP-owning companies show lower default rates and losses, but many SMEs do not yet use IP for financing.

#### How does patent collateral work in practice?

The patent owner grants a security interest (pledge, charge, or mortgage) over the patent to the lender, typically registered in the relevant patent or IP register. In default, the lender can enforce the security to recover the loan. Structures vary by jurisdiction; some countries have specific IP pledge registers. Lenders often require IP valuation, legal due diligence, and sometimes insurance. The process is less developed in Europe than in the US, where thousands of patents are pledged annually.

#### What are the barriers to IP-backed lending in Europe?

Barriers include: limited lender familiarity with IP; valuation and enforceability uncertainty; lack of liquid secondary markets for pledged IP; fragmented national registers and procedures; and conservative lending practices. The European Commission and national authorities are working on improving IP finance through awareness, valuation standards, and support for innovative financial products. Some banks (e.g. HSBC, NatWest in the UK) already offer IP-backed lending.

#### How does IP-backed financing support patent commercialisation?

IP-backed lending can fund R&D, scaling, and market expansion without diluting ownership. It can also support acquisitions, licensing programmes, and litigation. Better access to debt for IP-rich firms encourages further innovation and commercialisation. As European policies and practices mature, IP-backed financing is expected to play a larger role in the innovation economy.
