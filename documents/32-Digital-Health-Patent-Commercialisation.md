# Digital Health Patent Commercialisation

## Overview

This article covers patent commercialisation in the European digital health and health-tech sector.

---

#### What technologies are patentable in digital health in Europe?

Patentable subject matter includes: **diagnostic methods** – where they have technical character (e.g. devices, imaging); **medical devices** – software-controlled devices, wearables, implants; **algorithms** – when solving technical problems (e.g. image analysis, signal processing); **data processing** – for clinical decision support, where technical effect is present. Pure business methods or mental acts are excluded. The EPO examines digital health inventions under its software and medical technology guidelines.

#### What EU regulations affect digital health commercialisation?

Key regulations include: **MDR/IVDR** – Medical Devices and In Vitro Diagnostic Regulations; **GDPR** – for health data; **AI Act** – for AI-based medical devices (high-risk classification); **ePrivacy** – for electronic communications. Compliance affects product design, data flows, and market access. Patent strategy should align with regulatory classification; regulatory approval can enhance commercial value and licensing potential.

**References:**
- [European Commission – Medical devices](https://health.ec.europa.eu/medical-devices_en)
- [European Commission – AI Act](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai)

#### What licensing structures suit digital health in Europe?

Structures may combine: **patent licence** – for core technology; **software licence** – for algorithms and platforms; **data licence** – where applicable; **white-label or OEM** – embedding in partner products. Subscription or SaaS models affect royalty structures. B2B (e.g. hospitals, insurers) and B2C models differ in sales channels and agreements. MedTech companies such as Philips offer health technology IP licensing programmes.

#### How does the Unitary Patent benefit digital health commercialisation?

Digital health products often target multiple European markets. The Unitary Patent provides uniform protection across participating states with a single registration and renewal. The UPC offers centralised enforcement. For startups and SMEs scaling across the EU, this reduces cost and complexity. Patent portfolios covering software, devices, and methods can be consolidated under Unitary Patents where appropriate.

#### What role do universities and hospitals play in digital health patent commercialisation?

Universities and hospitals generate digital health innovations (e.g. decision support, imaging, wearables). TTOs manage IP and support licensing or spin-offs. EU-funded projects (e.g. Horizon Europe health topics) produce exploitable results. The Dermis Pharma case illustrates how strong IP and commercialisation expertise enable deals. Collaboration between clinicians, engineers, and TTOs is essential for translating research into products.
