# Patent Lifecycle and Commercialisation Timing

## Overview

This article discusses how patent lifecycle and timing affect commercialisation strategy in Europe.

---

#### How does the patent lifecycle affect commercialisation decisions?

The lifecycle includes: **filing** – priority date, claims; **examination** – grant or refusal; **opposition** – post-grant challenge (9 months at EPO); **maintenance** – renewal, pruning; **expiry** – end of term (typically 20 years from filing). Commercialisation timing: **pre-grant** – can licence pending applications, but higher risk; **post-grant** – stronger position, clearer scope; **late in term** – less remaining value, may affect royalty. Opposition and UPC validity proceedings can alter the lifecycle.

#### When is the best time to approach potential licensees?

Timing depends on: **technology readiness** – proof of concept, prototype, commercial product; **patent status** – pending vs granted; **market readiness** – demand, regulation; **competitor activity**. Early engagement can build relationships but may expose ideas. Later engagement with stronger data may improve terms but allow competitors to advance. Universities often engage after proof of concept and initial patent filing. Phased approaches (e.g. option then licence) can manage risk.

#### How does the 20-year patent term affect commercialisation strategy?

The 20-year term (from filing) limits exclusivity. Commercialisation strategy should consider: **time to market** – development, regulatory approval (especially pharma); **peak revenue period** – when to maximise licensing; **generic/biosimilar entry** – in pharma, post-patent competition. For technologies with long development (e.g. drugs), Supplementary Protection Certificates (SPCs) extend protection in Europe. Licensing early in term can maximise royalty duration.

#### What is the role of priority dates in commercialisation?

The priority date determines novelty and inventive step assessment. First-to-file systems (as in Europe) favour early filing. For commercialisation: **early filing** – secures priority, but may limit claim refinement; **delayed filing** – allows more development, but risks prior art. PCT and EP applications allow deferred national/regional phase entry, preserving priority while deferring cost. Strategic filing supports strong patents for licensing.

**References:**
- [EPO – Priority](https://www.epo.org/applying/european/guide-for-applicants/html/e/ga_a_4.html)
- [EPO – PCT](https://www.epo.org/applying/international-applications.html)

#### How do opposition and validity proceedings affect commercialisation timing?

EPO opposition (9-month window post-grant) can amend or revoke a patent. UPC and national revocation actions can occur later. Commercialisation may be delayed until opposition outcome; or parties may proceed with validity risk allocation in the licence. Licensees may seek validity warranties or adjustment clauses. Strong patents withstand challenge; weak ones may not. Validity assessment supports informed commercialisation decisions.
