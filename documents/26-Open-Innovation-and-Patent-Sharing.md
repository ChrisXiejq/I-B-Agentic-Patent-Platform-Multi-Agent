# Open Innovation and Patent Sharing

## Overview

This article explores open innovation, patent sharing, and collaborative models in European patent commercialisation.

---

#### What is open innovation and how does it relate to patents?

Open innovation involves combining internal and external ideas, technologies, and paths to market. For patents, this means: licensing-in external technology; licensing-out or sharing internal technology; co-development and joint ownership; participation in standards and patent pools; and defensive or strategic patent sharing. Open innovation can accelerate development and commercialisation but requires careful IP management to protect interests and manage risk.

#### What are patent pledges and how are they used in Europe?

Patent pledges are voluntary commitments by patent owners to limit enforcement (e.g. not to assert, or to license on specific terms). They are used in open source, standards, and sustainability contexts. Examples include Tesla’s patent pledge and various open-source–related pledges. In Europe, pledges may be less common than in the US but are gaining attention. Legal effect depends on wording and applicable law; they can support ecosystem development and goodwill.

#### How do patent pools support collaborative licensing?

Patent pools aggregate patents from multiple owners into a single licensing programme. They enable one-stop licensing, reduce transaction costs, and can accelerate technology adoption. In Europe, pools are common for SEPs (e.g. in telecoms, video). The EU TTBER recognises pro-competitive pools with transparent processes and independent evaluation. Pools balance collaboration with competition; participation decisions depend on strategic and commercial considerations.

#### What IP considerations apply to Horizon Europe collaborative projects?

Horizon Europe projects often involve multiple beneficiaries. IP considerations include: ownership of results (individual vs joint); access rights for implementation and exploitation; background and side-ground IP; licensing and transfer rules; and exploitation obligations. Consortium and Joint Ownership Agreements should address these. The grant agreement sets baseline rules; parties can agree more favourable terms among themselves.

#### How can open innovation support patent commercialisation in universities?

Universities increasingly engage in open innovation through: non-exclusive licensing for broad diffusion; participation in pre-competitive consortia; industry co-funding with shared IP; open-source-compatible licensing where appropriate. Balancing openness with revenue and impact requires clear IP policies. TTOs and ASTP support best practice. Open innovation can expand the pool of adopters and generate non-monetary impact (e.g. citations, collaborations).
