# Patent Portfolio Management

## Overview

This article covers strategies and practices for managing patent portfolios to support commercialisation in Europe.

---

#### What is patent portfolio management and why does it matter for commercialisation?

Portfolio management involves the strategic acquisition, maintenance, abandonment, and exploitation of patents. It aligns IP with business and commercialisation goals. Effective management: ensures resources focus on high-value patents; supports licensing and partnership discussions; reduces unnecessary renewal costs; identifies gaps and opportunities; and maintains FTO. For universities, companies, and research organisations, portfolio management is integral to translating R&D into market impact.

#### What decisions are involved in patent portfolio management?

Key decisions include: **filing** – which inventions to patent and where; **maintenance** – which patents to renew and for how long; **abandonment** – which to drop to save costs; **exploitation** – licensing, assignment, or internal use; **acquisition** – buying or licensing-in to fill gaps; **enforcement** – when to assert against infringers. These decisions should be based on commercial relevance, validity, cost, and strategic fit. Regular portfolio reviews (e.g. annual) support informed choices.

#### How can the Unitary Patent simplify portfolio management in Europe?

The Unitary Patent reduces administrative burden by: **single renewal** – one fee for all participating states; **single register** – centralised status and recordals; **centralised enforcement** – UPC for infringement and validity. For portfolios covering multiple European countries, consolidating to Unitary Patents can lower cost and simplify management. The decision to request unitary effect should consider market coverage, enforcement strategy, and opt-out implications.

#### What metrics support patent portfolio management?

Metrics may include: number of patents by technology, geography, and age; renewal rate and cost; licensing revenue and deal count; citation and quality indicators; alignment with products or business units; FTO coverage. Universities and PROs may track invention disclosures, filings, licences, and spin-offs. Metrics should support strategic questions: Is the portfolio aligned with commercialisation goals? Are resources well allocated?

#### How do joint ownership and consortium projects affect portfolio management?

Horizon Europe and other collaborative projects often produce jointly owned results. Joint owners must agree on: exploitation rights, licensing, assignment, and maintenance. Without clear agreements, joint ownership can complicate commercialisation. Consortium agreements and Joint Ownership Agreements should address these issues upfront. TTOs and legal advisors can help structure arrangements that enable effective portfolio management and exploitation.
