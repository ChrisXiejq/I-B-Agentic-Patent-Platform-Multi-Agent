# Patent Quality and Commercialisation Success

## Overview

This article explores the relationship between patent quality and successful commercialisation in Europe.

---

#### What defines patent quality in the commercialisation context?

Quality encompasses: **legal strength** – validity, enforceability, clear scope; **technical strength** – novelty, inventiveness, breadth of claims; **commercial relevance** – alignment with products or markets; **strategic fit** – portfolio coherence. High-quality patents are easier to license, enforce, and defend. The EPO aims to grant patents that meet high legal and technical standards. Quality assessment supports commercialisation prioritisation.

#### How does patent quality affect licensing outcomes?

Licensees prefer patents with: strong validity (lower risk of invalidation); clear scope (predictable freedom to operate); relevance to their products. Weak patents may receive lower royalties or no interest. Due diligence often includes validity assessment. Quality signals (e.g. EPO grant, citation metrics) can support negotiation. Universities and PROs can improve outcomes by filing strategically and ensuring claims align with commercial applications.

#### What role does the EPO play in patent quality?

The EPO conducts rigorous search and examination. High-quality examination reduces post-grant challenges and supports legal certainty. The EPO provides examiner guidelines, quality management, and cooperation with national offices. Opposition proceedings allow third parties to challenge validity. EPO-quality patents are generally more attractive for commercialisation and enforcement across Europe.

**References:**
- [EPO – Patent quality](https://www.epo.org/about-us/patent-quality.html)

#### How can patent quality be assessed before commercialisation?

Assessment methods include: **claim analysis** – scope, clarity, potential design-arounds; **prior art search** – novelty and inventive step; **validity opinion** – from patent attorney; **citation and metrics** – forward citations, technological relevance; **commercial review** – alignment with products and markets. Combined assessment supports go/no-go decisions and valuation. Early quality focus at filing improves downstream commercialisation.

**References:**

#### How does the UPC affect patent quality considerations?

The UPC provides centralised validity proceedings. A single revocation action can affect a patent across the UPC territory. Patent quality matters more when validity can be challenged in one proceeding with broad effect. Strong patents are better positioned; weak patents face higher risk. Commercialisation strategy (including licensing and enforcement) should account for UPC validity risk.
