# Improvements and Grant-Back Clauses

## Overview

This article explains how improvement and grant-back provisions work in European patent licensing.

---

#### What are improvement and grant-back clauses?

**Improvements** are modifications or developments of the licensed technology. **Grant-back** obliges the licensee to license improvements back to the licensor. Clauses may require: **non-exclusive grant-back** – licensor may use the improvement; **exclusive grant-back** – can raise competition concerns; **reciprocal** – both parties grant back; **scope** – what counts as an improvement (narrow vs broad). These clauses affect innovation incentives and competitive dynamics. EU competition rules (TTBER) address exclusive grant-backs.

#### How do EU competition rules treat grant-back clauses?

The TTBER and Guidelines distinguish: **non-exclusive grant-back** – generally acceptable; **exclusive grant-back** – may fall outside the block exemption if it forecloses the licensee from exploiting its own improvement. The Commission assesses the competitive impact. Non-exclusive grant-backs preserve licensee incentives while allowing the licensor to benefit. Exclusive grant-backs can weaken licensee innovation and reduce competition. Legal advice is recommended for grant-back design.

#### What should improvement clauses define?

Key definitions: **improvement** – based on the licensed technology; **ownership** – who owns the improvement (often the developer); **disclosure** – whether licensee must inform licensor; **grant-back** – scope (non-exclusive preferred for competition compliance), territory, royalty; **sublicensing** – whether licensor may sublicense the improvement. Ambiguity can lead to disputes. Clear definitions and procedures (e.g. for disclosure, negotiation) support smooth operation.

#### How do improvements affect royalty and valuation?

Improvements can increase the value of the licensed technology over time. Licence agreements may: **fixed royalty** – no change for improvements; **renegotiation** – periodic review; **separate licence** – for significant improvements. Grant-back may be royalty-free or for consideration. In Horizon Europe and joint projects, improvement handling is often addressed in consortium and Joint Ownership Agreements. Valuation of improvements can be complex; expert input may be needed.

#### What are best practices for improvement clauses in university licences?

Universities often retain rights to publish and conduct research. Improvement clauses should: preserve academic freedom where possible; define improvements in a way that does not capture unrelated research; use non-exclusive grant-back to comply with competition law; address ownership of improvements arising from joint research. TTOs negotiate to balance commercial and academic interests. Industry may seek broad improvement rights; universities may seek to limit scope.
