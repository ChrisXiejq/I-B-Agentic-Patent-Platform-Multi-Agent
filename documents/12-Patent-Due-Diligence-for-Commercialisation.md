# Patent Due Diligence for Commercialisation

## Overview

This article explains the purpose, scope, and key elements of patent due diligence in European commercialisation transactions.

---

#### What is patent due diligence and when is it needed?

Patent due diligence is a structured review of the patent portfolio involved in a transaction—licensing, M&A, investment, or collaboration. It assesses ownership, validity, scope, freedom to operate (FTO), encumbrances, and commercial relevance. Due diligence is typically needed before signing material agreements, completing acquisitions, securing investment, or entering joint ventures. It helps identify risks and inform pricing and deal structure.

#### What are the main elements of patent due diligence in Europe?

Key elements include: **ownership and chain of title** – confirming the seller/licensor has rights and that assignments and employee agreements are in order; **validity** – assessing novelty, inventive step, and susceptibility to opposition or invalidity; **scope** – understanding claim coverage and relevance to products; **encumbrances** – identifying licences, pledges, or other restrictions; **freedom to operate** – third-party patent risks; **maintenance** – renewal status and costs; **territory** – countries covered (including Unitary Patent where applicable).

#### How does the EPO Patent Register support due diligence?

The European Patent Register provides legal status information for European applications and patents, including: publication dates, procedural steps, oppositions, appeals, assignments, licences (when recorded), and renewal status. For Unitary Patents, the Unitary Patent Register offers similar information. Registers in designated states may also need to be checked for national validations and encumbrances.

#### What are common risks identified in patent due diligence?

Risks may include: unclear or disputed ownership; co-ownership or joint development without clear agreements; prior licences that limit exclusivity or territorial scope; patent pledges or security interests; validity challenges (opposition, invalidity actions); narrow or expiring claims; third-party FTO issues; failure to pay renewal fees; and Horizon Europe or other grant obligations that restrict transfer or licensing. Addressing these early can avoid costly disputes.

#### How can due diligence findings affect deal terms?

Findings can influence: **pricing** – lower valuation if validity or scope is weak; **representations and warranties** – specific disclosures and carve-outs; **indemnities** – allocation of risk for identified issues; **conditions precedent** – e.g. resolving ownership before closing; **structure** – excluding certain patents or territories; **post-closing obligations** – maintaining patents, managing oppositions. Thorough due diligence supports informed negotiation and risk allocation.
