# Basic Concepts of Patent Commercialisation

## Overview

This article explores the core concepts, legal foundations, and value propositions of patent commercialisation in the European context.

---

#### What is patent commercialisation?

Patent commercialisation is the process of bringing patented technology from the laboratory to the market, encompassing implementation, licensing, assignment, equity participation, and other forms of exploitation. It transforms intellectual property into economic and social value. Under European and national patent laws, commercialisation may take the form of direct implementation, licensing to third parties, outright assignment, contribution to capital, or collaborative development.

#### How do European universities commercialise their patents?

European universities and public research organisations bring over one-third of their inventions to market, with another 42% planned for exploitation. Licensing is the dominant channel, accounting for about 70% of commercialised inventions, followed by R&D cooperation (14%) and patent sales (9%). Universities increasingly retain ownership of patented inventions and take on proactive commercialisation, often through dedicated knowledge transfer offices (KTOs).

#### What are the main stages of patent commercialisation?

Key stages include: (1) **Screening and valuation** – identifying high-value patents and assessing commercial potential; (2) **Market matching** – connecting patent owners with potential licensees via platforms, fairs, or intermediaries; (3) **Deal negotiation** – defining licence/assignment terms, compensation, and contractual clauses; (4) **Rights transfer** – recording assignments or licence agreements with patent offices; (5) **Implementation** – adoption by the licensee in products or processes; (6) **Post-deal management** – support, revenue sharing, and dispute handling. All stages must align to maximise conversion.

#### What is the policy framework for patent commercialisation in the EU?

The EU and Member States support commercialisation through research and innovation programmes (e.g. Horizon Europe), IP strategies, and national innovation policies. Horizon Europe beneficiaries must use their best efforts to exploit project results for four years after the project ends. The European Commission offers services such as the IP Helpdesk and Horizon IP Scan to support SMEs and research organisations in IP management and exploitation.

#### What value does patent commercialisation create?

Commercialisation generates **economic value** through licensing income, sales proceeds, and product revenues; **industrial value** by advancing technology and strengthening patent-intensive industries; **social value** through employment, better products, and competitiveness; and **innovation ecosystem value** by rewarding R&D and enabling a virtuous cycle of research, exploitation, and reinvestment. Effective commercialisation of high-value patents can multiply benefits across supply chains and sectors.
