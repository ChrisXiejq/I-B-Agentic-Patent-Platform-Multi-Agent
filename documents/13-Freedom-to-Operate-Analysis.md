# Freedom to Operate Analysis

## Overview

This article describes freedom to operate (FTO) analysis and its role in European patent commercialisation and product launch decisions.

---

#### What is Freedom to Operate (FTO) analysis?

Freedom to operate analysis assesses whether a product, process, or service can be developed, manufactured, or sold without infringing third-party patent rights. It typically involves identifying relevant patents in target markets, evaluating their scope and validity, and determining infringement risk. FTO is distinct from patentability (whether an invention can be patented) and is essential before significant commercial investment or market entry.

**References:**
- [EPO – Patent searching](https://www.epo.org/searching-for-patents.html)
- [European Commission – IP Helpdesk](https://intellectual-property-helpdesk.ec.europa.eu/)

---

#### When should FTO analysis be conducted?

FTO is typically conducted: **before R&D** – to steer development away from blocking patents; **before product launch** – to assess market entry risk; **before licensing or acquisition** – to understand portfolio strength and gaps; **before investment** – to inform investor due diligence; **in response to allegations** – when a third party claims infringement. Early FTO can avoid expensive redesign or litigation; late FTO may expose the company to injunctions and damages.

#### What tools and databases support FTO in Europe?

The EPO provides Espacenet for patent search, the Patent Register for legal status, and Patent Analytics for landscape analysis. Commercial tools add claim mapping, citation analysis, and workflow support. For European markets, searches should cover EPO applications, national patents, and (where relevant) Unitary Patents. Competitor portfolios and key technology areas should be systematically reviewed. Professional search and legal analysis are often needed for high-stakes decisions.

**References:**

- [EPO – Patent Register](https://register.epo.org/)

#### What are the possible outcomes of FTO analysis?

Outcomes may include: **clear** – no significant blocking patents identified; **low risk** – patents exist but appear invalid, expired, or non-infringed; **medium risk** – some patents require monitoring or design-around; **high risk** – blocking patents require licensing, invalidity action, or design change. Risk mitigation options include: licensing, invalidation, design-around, acquiring rights, or avoiding the market. Documenting the analysis supports informed decision-making and may be relevant in litigation.

**References:**

#### How does the Unitary Patent and UPC affect FTO in Europe?

The Unitary Patent provides uniform protection across participating EU states; a single patent can block activities in all those countries. The UPC offers centralised infringement and validity proceedings. FTO analysis must now consider Unitary Patents and the risk of UPC litigation, which can affect the entire UPC territory. Conversely, a single UPC validity ruling can clear or invalidate a patent across that territory, simplifying FTO for implementers.
