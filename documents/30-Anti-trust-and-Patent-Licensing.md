# Anti-trust and Patent Licensing

## Overview

This article outlines EU competition law considerations for patent licensing and commercialisation.

---

#### What EU competition rules apply to patent licensing?

Article 101 TFEU prohibits agreements that restrict competition. Patent licences may fall under this if they contain clauses that restrict competition (e.g. territorial restrictions, output limitations, resale price maintenance). The Technology Transfer Block Exemption Regulation (TTBER) provides a safe harbour for agreements that meet its conditions. Hardcore restrictions (e.g. price fixing, market allocation between competitors) are not exempted. Agreements outside the safe harbour require individual assessment.

#### What is the Technology Transfer Block Exemption Regulation (TTBER)?

The TTBER exempts certain technology transfer agreements (including patent licences) from Article 101(1) TFEU. It applies to bilateral agreements between two parties, subject to market share thresholds (generally 20% for competitors, 30% for non-competitors). The accompanying Guidelines explain the application, including to patent pools, licensing in settlement contexts, and vertical agreements. Agreements that exceed thresholds or include hardcore restrictions fall outside the block exemption.

#### What licence terms may raise competition concerns?

Terms that may raise concerns include: **territorial restrictions** – limiting where the licensee may sell (sometimes acceptable for non-competitors); **field-of-use restrictions** – may be acceptable if they do not foreclose competition; **exclusive dealing** – tying licence to exclusive supply; **grant-back** – obligations to assign or license improvements (exclusive grant-back can be problematic); **no-challenge** – restricting licensee’s right to challenge validity (void in the EU); **bundling** – tying licence to unwanted technology. The TTBER Guidelines provide detailed guidance.

#### How does abuse of dominance affect patent licensing?

Article 102 TFEU prohibits abuse of a dominant position. For patent holders, concerns may arise from: **refusal to licence** – in exceptional circumstances, refusal may be abusive; **excessive pricing** – royalties far above FRAND or competitive level; **tying** – conditioning licence on purchase of other products; **discrimination** – dissimilar conditions for equivalent transactions. SEP holders have particular obligations; the Commission and courts have addressed FRAND and injunctions in SEP cases.

#### What role does the Commission play in patent and competition?

The Commission enforces EU competition rules, including in technology sectors. It has investigated SEP licensing, standardisation, and patent settlements (e.g. in pharma). The proposed EU SEP regulation would add tools for transparency and FRAND dispute resolution. Parties to licence agreements should consider competition compliance as part of commercialisation strategy. Legal advice is recommended for complex or high-value arrangements.
