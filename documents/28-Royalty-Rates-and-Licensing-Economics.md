# Royalty Rates and Licensing Economics

## Overview

This article discusses royalty rate determination and licensing economics in European patent commercialisation.

---

#### What factors influence patent royalty rates in Europe?

Factors include: **technology value** – novelty, market potential, competitive advantage; **market size** – addressable market and growth; **licence scope** – exclusivity, territory, field of use; **stage of development** – early vs commercial stage; **bargaining power** – relative position of licensor and licensee; **industry norms** – sector-specific benchmarks (e.g. pharma vs software); **risk** – technical, regulatory, market. Royalty stacking in complex products (e.g. mobile devices) affects cumulative rates.

#### What are common royalty structures in European licensing?

Structures include: **running royalty** – percentage of net sales or revenue; **lump sum** – one-time payment; **hybrid** – upfront plus royalty; **tiered royalty** – rate changes with volume; **minimum royalty** – floor regardless of sales; **milestone payments** – on development or regulatory events (common in pharma). Choice depends on risk allocation, cash flow, and commercial objectives. FRAND licences for SEPs often use running royalties with caps or floors.

#### How are industry benchmark royalty rates used?

Benchmarks come from: **comparable licences** – reported or known deals in the field; **industry surveys** – e.g. licensing royalty rate studies; **court decisions** – reasonable royalty in damages cases; **25% rule (historical)** – licensor’s share of licensee’s profit; now often criticised. Benchmarks provide a starting point but must be adjusted for specific circumstances. For SEPs, FRAND rates are disputed; courts and regulators are developing approaches.

#### What is the income approach to royalty rate determination?

The income approach estimates the incremental profit or cost savings attributable to the licensed technology. The royalty is set to share that value between licensor and licensee. Steps: project revenue and costs with and without the technology; estimate incremental profit; allocate a share to the licensor (e.g. 25–50%); express as royalty rate. Sensitivity to assumptions is high; discount rates and risk adjustments affect results. Used for valuation and negotiation support.

#### How do royalty audits work in European licensing?

Licence agreements often include audit rights: the licensor (or its auditor) may inspect the licensee’s records to verify royalty calculations. Audits typically occur on notice, with frequency limits (e.g. once per year), and the licensee may be required to correct underpayments and sometimes pay interest. Costs may be borne by the licensor unless underpayment exceeds a threshold. Audit clauses support compliance and trust in long-term licence relationships.
